Storm Basics

A storm is any disturbed state of the atmosphere that affects the Earth’s surface. Storms can bring rain, snow, wind, lightning, hail, or extreme heat. They form when warm, moist air rises and meets cooler air, creating instability and energy in the atmosphere.

Why Storms Matter

Storms matter because they affect:

  • Homes and buildings
  • Roads, bridges, and transportation
  • Schools, hospitals, and emergency services
  • Power, water, and communication systems
  • Wildlife and the environment
  • People’s safety and health

Types of Storms (Expanded)

Floods: The most common natural disaster worldwide.

Thunderstorms: Bring lightning, hail, and strong winds.

Hurricanes: Massive rotating storms with storm surge.

Tornadoes: Spinning columns of destructive wind.

Blizzards: Snowstorms with freezing temperatures.

Heat Waves: Long periods of extreme heat.

Derechos: Long-lasting windstorms.

Dust Storms: Huge walls of dust that reduce visibility.

Firestorms: Fires so intense they create their own wind.

How Storms Form (Expanded)

Storms form when warm, moist air rises and meets cooler air. This rising air cools and condenses into clouds. If there is enough moisture and energy, the clouds grow taller and stronger, turning into storms.

Hurricanes need warm ocean water and low wind shear. Tornadoes often form from rotating thunderstorms called supercells. Blizzards need cold air, moisture, and strong winds. Heat waves form when high‑pressure systems trap hot air over an area for days or weeks.

Climate Change & Storms

Climate change increases:

  • Ocean temperatures → stronger hurricanes
  • Air moisture → heavier rainfall
  • Heat waves → more deaths
  • Droughts → more wildfire storms
  • Sea levels → worse flooding
